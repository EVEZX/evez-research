### UP Employee Health & Medical Testing
- Union Pacific, like all Class I railroads, contracts with **occupational health providers** for employee medical services.
- **Concentra Health Services** (now publicly traded as CON, majority-owned by Select Medical) is the dominant occupational health provider in the U.S. — treating 1 in 7 occupational injuries nationally with 627 centers across 47 states.
- Concentra was founded in 1979 and is headquartered in Addison, Texas. It is the most likely provider for UP employee medical testing, though UP may also use other occupational medicine providers or in-house medical protocols.
- **Who controls Steven's medical testing:** If Steven was a UP employee (or contractor), his chemical exposure testing would have been controlled by:
  1. **UP's Occupational Health / Medical Department** (corporate-level, Omaha HQ)
  2. **UP's contracted occupational health provider** (likely Concentra or similar)
  3. **UP's Employee Assistance Program (EAP)**
  4. UP's Environmental & Safety department
- Railroad workers are covered under the **Federal Employers' Liability Act (FELA)** for workplace injuries, NOT standard workers' compensation. This means:
  - Medical testing and treatment decisions are controlled by the railroad's medical department
  - Workers must prove negligence to recover damages
  - The railroad has significant control over which doctors and tests are authorized
- **FOIA/Discovery targets:** UP Medical Department protocols, Concentra/contractor medical records, UP Environmental Health & Safety incident reports

### UP I-80 Operations
- The **Evanston Subdivision** is the UP rail line that parallels I-80 through Uinta County. This is a major transcontinental freight corridor.
- Evanston has historic UP infrastructure including the Union Pacific Railroad Complex (the "roundhouse").
- Any chemical event on or near the UP tracks in the Evanston area would trigger UP's hazmat response protocols.

- **Current Assistant Secretary:** David Keeling (head of OSHA)

- **Critical:** Wyoming is one of **22 states/territories with an OSHA-approved state plan**. Wyoming operates its own OSHA program (Wyoming OSHA / WYOSHA), which must be "at least as effective as federal OSHA."

## 14. Assessment

The chain of authority for both traffic direction and medical testing runs through identifiable command structures:

**Traffic through plume:** WHP (Colonel Tim Cameron → troop commander → on-scene troopers) with WYDOT 511 support. The decision to route traffic through a chemical plume rather than around it would have been made within this chain. The absence of any public press release suggests this was handled at the field level without headquarters-level public communication.

**Medical testing control:** UP Medical Department (corporate, Omaha) → contracted occupational health provider (likely Concentra) → with Wyoming OSHA as the regulatory backstop (but only if triggered). FELA gives the railroad significant control over medical documentation, creating an inherent conflict of interest when the railroad is both the party responsible for the exposure and the party controlling the medical evidence.

The regulatory framework (FRA, NTSB, OSHA, EPA) provides oversight but with significant gaps: NTSB can only recommend, FRA doesn't manage medical testing, OSHA requires a complaint/inspection trigger, and EPA focuses on environmental (not occupational) exposure. This creates a regulatory void where the railroad itself becomes the primary authority over documenting whether its own workers were harmed.
