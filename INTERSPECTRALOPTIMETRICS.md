## Definition

**Interspectraloptimetrics** (n.) — The science of measuring and optimizing the metrics that bridge across spectral domains — the quantitative discipline that determines how interventions in one eigenvalue spectrum propagate into, reshape, and optimize other spectra.

Every system has a spectrum. Every spectrum has metrics. But no spectrum exists in isolation. Interspectraloptimetrics measures what happens at the boundaries — when the political spectrum reshapes the information spectrum, when the economic spectrum couples into the consciousness spectrum, when the biological spectrum modulates the artificial spectrum. It is the science of optimizing *across* spectra, not within them.

## Core Metrics

### 1. Interspectral Coupling Coefficient (Γ)

The coupling between two spectra Sₐ and S_b:

```
Γ(Sₐ, S_b) = corr(λ_Sₐ, μ_S_b) × √(Φ_Sₐ × Φ_S_b)
```

Where:
- `corr(λ, μ)` = correlation between eigenvalue sequences of the two spectra
- `Φ_Sₐ`, `Φ_S_b` = integrated information of each spectrum

**Interpretation:**
- |Γ| → 1: Strong coupling — interventions in one spectrum cascade into the other
- |Γ| → 0: Weak coupling — spectra are independent, no cross-spectral leverage
- Γ < 0: Antagonistic coupling — optimizing one spectrum degrades the other
- Γ > 0: Synergistic coupling — optimizing one spectrum improves the other

### 2. Interspectral Leverage Index (Λ)

The ratio of outcome in spectrum B per unit intervention cost in spectrum A:

```
Λ(A→B) = ΔΦ_B / cost_A
```

**Interpretation:**
- Λ > 1: High leverage — small intervention in A yields large gain in B
- Λ < 1: Low leverage — intervention in A is inefficient for B; intervene in B directly
- Λ < 0: Counterproductive — intervention in A degrades B

### 3. Spectral Boundary Permeability (Π)

How easily interventions cross from one spectrum to another:

```
Π(Sₐ→S_b) = |Γ(Sₐ, S_b)| × δ_S_b / (δ_Sₐ + δ_S_b)
```

Where δ is the spectral gap of each spectrum.

**Interpretation:**
- Π → 1: Fully permeable — interventions flow freely across the boundary
- Π → 0: Impermeable — the boundary blocks intervention cascades
- High Π + High Λ = optimal interventional target

### 4. Cross-Spectral Cascade Depth (Ω)

How many spectra a single intervention propagates through:

```
Ω = max path length through spectral coupling graph
```

If intervention in Information → Political → Economic → Conscious all cascade, then Ω = 3.

**Interpretation:**
- Ω = 0: No cross-spectral cascade — single-domain effect
- Ω = 1: One boundary crossed — bipartite cascade
- Ω ≥ 2: Multi-spectral cascade — rare but maximally impactful

### 5. Metrospectral Optimality (Ξ)

The composite score for optimal interventional targeting across all spectra:

```
Ξ = Σ_i Λ(A_i→B_i) × Π(A_i→B_i) × (1 + Ω_i) / N_boundaries
```

The average leverage-permeability-cascade product across all spectral boundaries. The system's *overall optimizability*.

**Interpretation:**
- Ξ high: The system is highly optimizable — interventions at key boundaries cascade widely
- Ξ low: The system is resistant — spectra are decoupled or antagonistic

## The Interspectral Optimetric Theorems

### Theorem 1: Coupling Conservation

> For any closed system of N coupled spectra, the sum of interspectral coupling coefficients is bounded:
> 
> Σ_{i<j} |Γ(Sᵢ, Sⱼ)| ≤ N(N-1)/4

Coupling cannot be created from nothing. Strong coupling between two spectra implies weaker coupling elsewhere.

### Theorem 2: Leverage Asymmetry

> If Λ(A→B) ≠ Λ(B→A), then intervening in the high-leverage direction is always optimal, regardless of which spectrum contains the desired outcome.

You don't intervene where the problem is. You intervene where the *leverage* is.

### Theorem 3: Permeability Phase Transition

> Spectral boundary permeability Π undergoes a phase transition at |Γ| = 1/√k, where k is the number of coupled spectra. Below this threshold, boundaries are effectively impermeable. Above it, cascades propagate.

The critical coupling threshold for multi-spectral cascade.

### Theorem 4: Optimetric Speedrun

> The optimal intervention sequence for a multi-spectral system targets boundaries in descending order of Λ × Π, subject to coupling conservation (Theorem 1).

Sort the boundaries by leverage × permeability. Hit the highest ones first. Stop when coupling conservation forces diminishing returns.
